The basics

What is anxiety?

Anxiety is the body's natural response to stress — a feeling of fear or apprehension about what's to come. It's a normal and often healthy emotion. When a person regularly feels disproportionate levels of anxiety, it might become a disorder.

Anxiety disorders form a category of mental health diagnoses that lead to excessive nervousness, fear, apprehension, and worry. These disorders alter how a person processes emotions and behaves, also causing physical symptoms.

Mild anxiety might be vague and unsettling, while severe anxiety may seriously affect day-to-day living. Anxiety disorders are the most common form of emotional disorder and can affect anyone at any age.

Types

Common anxiety disorders.

Recognizing the signs

Common symptoms.

Anxiety can manifest in both physical and emotional ways. Recognizing them is the first step.

  • Feeling nervous, restless, or tense — a constant sense of dread or unease
  • Increased heart rate and rapid breathing, even without physical exertion
  • Sweating, trembling, or feeling weak in the knees
  • Trouble concentrating — the mind going blank or fixating on worry
  • Gastrointestinal issues such as nausea or upset stomach
  • Difficulty sleeping — lying awake with racing thoughts
  • Avoiding triggers: people, places, or situations that provoke anxiety
  • A sense of impending danger, panic, or catastrophe

Finding relief

Coping strategies.

Small, consistent practices can dramatically shift how anxiety feels in the body and mind.

Breathing Photo <img src="YOUR_LINK_HERE" />

Deep breathing

Slow, deliberate breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system, signaling safety to your body and quieting the anxiety response.

Meditation Photo <img src="YOUR_LINK_HERE" />

Mindfulness & meditation

Observing thoughts without judgment loosens their grip. Even 10 minutes a day of mindful sitting can reduce anxiety over time.

Movement Photo <img src="YOUR_LINK_HERE" />

Physical movement

Exercise releases endorphins and burns off adrenaline — nature's most effective anxiety reducer. Even a 20-minute walk helps.

Journaling Photo <img src="YOUR_LINK_HERE" />

Journaling

Writing out worries externalizes them. When anxiety lives only in your head, it feels endless. On paper, it becomes something manageable.

Seeking support

When to get help.

If anxiety is interfering with your relationships, work, or daily life — or if you're using alcohol or substances to cope — it's time to speak with a professional.

Effective treatments exist, including Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), medication, or a combination of both. Reaching out is a sign of strength, not weakness.

You are not alone. Millions of people manage anxiety successfully with the right support.
